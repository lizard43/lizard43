91363 RGB Interface Board

Astrocade color-difference video decoder, RGB matrix, sync output, and CRT monitor interface

A082-91363-B000 RGB Interface TBA530 Matrix No Hot-Plugging
⟵ 90708 Game Board ⌂ 91354 CPU Board ⟶

The 91363 RGB Interface Board is the small Bally/Midway Astrocade video interface board that converts the card-rack video outputs into monitor-ready analog red, green, blue, and negative composite sync.

The Astrocade arcade card rack does not feed the color monitor as plain RGB. The interface board receives three native video signals on its 12-pin input connector: R-Y, B-Y, and VIDEO. The board delays and biases the two color-difference channels, mixes them with the video/luminance path in U1, and sends separate R, G, B, and -SYNC signals to the monitor-side 9-pin connector.

Factory service warning Do not unplug or insert this PCB with the game powered. The loose warning tag in the reference photo is right: hot-plugging the board or monitor harness can damage the interface board, the game board, or the monitor input circuitry.
Review correction The two large tan cylinders are not power resistors. The factory component drawing identifies them as DL1 and DL2, both 270 ns delay lines. The main IC is U1 TBA530, an RGB matrix / pre-amplifier, not an LM3900 quad op-amp.

Board identity

Factory drawing

The attached component drawing and schematic title blocks identify the circuit as RGB INTERFACE for CCRS and show drawing / board number A082-91363-B000.

Board role

It is not a CPU board, pattern board, or game logic card. It is the cabinet video conversion board between the Astrocade card-rack video harness and the color CRT monitor harness.

Board family

Use 91363 as the family identifier. Original boards and service literature may show suffixes such as A000 or B000; compare the actual schematic, connector keying, and harness before swapping parts.

Connector format

The component drawing labels the monitor-side connector as J1 KK-100-9 and the card-rack side as J2 KK-100-12.

Board architecture and signal jobs

×

Color-difference inputs

J2 pin 9 brings in R-Y and runs through DL2. J2 pin 7 brings in B-Y and runs through DL1. Both delay lines are 270 ns parts.

RGB matrix

U1 TBA530 combines the delayed color-difference paths with the video/luminance path. Its outputs route to J1 pin 2 red, J1 pin 5 green, and J1 pin 7 blue.

Sync extraction

J2 pin 11 is the incoming VIDEO signal. It feeds both the video/matrix path and the separate sync path around Q1 2N4401, producing negative composite sync at J1 pin 9.

Power rails and returns

The input connector supplies +12V, +5V, and -5V. Multiple ground pins serve as power and video/sync returns; keep the paired returns with their signals.

Do not copy the common bad pinout J2 pin 1 is ground on the schematic, not video. VIDEO enters on J2 pin 11. J2 pin 6 is the connector key position, not a logic input.

Monitor hookup notes

These notes are for field verification only. The 91363 side is well defined by the factory schematic, but CRT chassis input headers vary by monitor model, daughtercard, revision, and harness orientation.

CRT safety Unplug the cabinet before service. A color CRT monitor must be powered through the cabinet's proper isolation transformer. Do not defeat the isolation transformer, do not move connectors with power on, and verify the chassis input header before power-up.

91363 side

Use J1 pin 2 for red, J1 pin 5 for green, J1 pin 7 for blue, J1 pin 9 for negative composite sync, and one or more J1 ground pins for video/sync return.

Monitor side

Match those signals to the exact monitor input board. Some chassis families have separate positive-sync and negative-sync inputs, and some Wells-Gardner K4600 interface cards move negative sync away from the main RGB header.

Do not trust wire color alone

Wire colors are useful clues, but old cabinet harnesses are often repaired, extended, or replaced. Verify pin numbers and continuity.

Do not one-pin offset

The input connector carries power rails and the output connector feeds the monitor input. A shifted connector can put the wrong voltage or signal on the wrong node.

Diagnostic and maintenance notes

  • One color missing: Check whether the missing color is absent at J1. If it is absent there, trace the related U1 output path and the corresponding input path. If it is present at J1, move downstream to the monitor header, neck board, chassis solder joints, and wiring.
  • Color edge shifted or smeared: Inspect DL1 and DL2, their solder joints, and their surrounding resistors. A delay-line or connection fault can make chroma information appear horizontally displaced from luminance edges.
  • Rolling, tearing, or no lock: Confirm that J1 pin 9 reaches the monitor's negative-sync input and that J1 pin 8 or another ground return is present where the monitor expects it.
  • Washed-out or unstable video: Verify ground continuity on the video/sync returns. A missing return in one of the signal pairs can look like a bad analog board.
  • Board damage after service: Recheck connector orientation and keying. J2 includes +12V, +5V, and -5V; offsetting that connector can put supply rails on signal pins.
Crosshatch interpretation A stable crosshatch with bad color points toward RGB level, matrix, wiring, or monitor color paths. A rolling or tearing crosshatch points first toward sync routing, sync polarity, or monitor sync input selection.

Modern repair and FPGA context

Modern Astrocade cabinet projects sometimes keep the original 9-pin monitor-side convention so a reproduction interface, test fixture, or FPGA bridge can plug into cabinet wiring without modifying the monitor harness. That is useful, but it is not always the same electrical job as the original 91363 circuit.

Original 91363 job

Decode Astrocade color-difference/video signals into analog RGB and negative sync for a commercial raster CRT.

Modern bridge job

Often maps already-standard RGB or FPGA-generated video into the cabinet's expected connector layout and power/interface conventions.
