Sound

The Astrocade custom I/O chip is both a sound generator and part of the machine's input/output story. Treat the sound registers as simple, but treat the arcade wiring as suspicious until proven.

Why sound is tricky

The simple part is the sound generator: eight write registers control a master oscillator, three tone channels, vibrato, tone volumes, noise modulation, and noise volume. The weird part is that this is not only a sound chip. MAME describes the Astrocade custom I/O chip as a sound chip whose read-side strobes and SI lines are also part of the input/output system.

On the home Astrocade, it is tempting to think of the sound/I/O lines as one neat console input matrix. On the arcade machines, that mental model breaks. The arcade games route SI inputs through external multiplexers and use SO strobes for unrelated outputs. That means a home-console sound trick that reads a port or assumes SO/SI symmetry can do the wrong thing on Wizard of Wor/Gorf-class arcade hardware.

The rule for this chapter Writes to the eight sound registers are the safe center. Reads and SO/SI assumptions belong to the input and cabinet-wiring world, not to a generic sound recipe.

MAME sound register map

MAME's Astrocade sound device gives the cleanest register-level description. The field-guide convention below numbers the chip registers as 0-7 and also shows the arcade port numbers used by our HVGLIB include.

Reg Port HVGLIB alias Purpose Bit meaning
0 $10 PORT_TONMO / TONMO Master oscillator frequency 8-bit frequency/rate value. MAME treats the reload value as negative logic internally.
1 $11 PORT_TONEA / TONEA Tone generator A frequency 8-bit tone A frequency value.
2 $12 PORT_TONEB / TONEB Tone generator B frequency 8-bit tone B frequency value.
3 $13 PORT_TONEC / TONEC Tone generator C frequency 8-bit tone C frequency value.
4 $14 PORT_VIBRA / VIBRA Vibrato D7-D6 vibrato speed, D5-D0 vibrato depth.
5 $15 PORT_VOLC / VOLC Noise/mux/tone C volume D5 noise AM enable, D4 mux source, D3-D0 tone C volume.
6 $16 PORT_VOLAB / VOLAB Tone B/A volume D7-D4 tone B volume, D3-D0 tone A volume.
7 $17 PORT_VOLN / VOLN Noise volume 8-bit noise volume; MAME contributes the upper nibble to audible noise level.
Port direction matters The same numeric port range overlaps the input map. OUT ($11),A writes tone A. IN A,($11) reads the player-2/input-mux side on WoW-class hardware. Never document a port without saying read or write.

HVGLIB names and the arcade include

The arcade HVGLIB include deliberately keeps console-style sound names where they are useful, while warning that the same numeric range is also used by input reads. For sound writes, use the output-side aliases.

Tone model: master oscillator plus A/B/C

The chip has three square-wave tone generators. The master oscillator controls the timing domain that clocks those tone generators. Each tone channel has its own 8-bit frequency register, and each channel contributes to the mixed output only when its square wave is in the active state and its volume nibble is nonzero.

Channel Frequency register Volume control Useful role
A reg 1 / $11 low nibble of reg 6 / VOLAB simple beeps, coin-up tone, melody lead.
B reg 2 / $12 high nibble of reg 6 / VOLAB second pitch, intervals, warnings, two-tone effects.
C reg 3 / $13 low nibble of reg 5 / VOLC third pitch, drones, or a channel to combine with noise modulation.
Practical tuning rule Start with one channel, one volume nibble, and a known note constant. Once that works, add the second channel. Most confusing sound bugs come from changing several frequency, volume, and modulation registers at once.

Volume and mixing registers

The chip does not have one global volume register. Channel volume is split across registers 5 and 6, and noise level is in register 7.

Register Bits Meaning Example
reg 5 / $15 D3-D0 Tone C volume, 0-15. $0F = tone C loud, no noise AM, mux=0.
reg 5 / $15 D4 Mux source: MAME comments describe 0 as vibrato, 1 as noise for master reload modulation. $10 selects noise source path with tone C volume 0.
reg 5 / $15 D5 Noise AM enable. $20 enables noise contribution/amplitude modulation path.
reg 6 / $16 D3-D0 Tone A volume, 0-15. $0F = A loud, B silent.
reg 6 / $16 D7-D4 Tone B volume, 0-15. $F0 = B loud, A silent.
reg 7 / $17 D7-D0 Noise volume/control value. $F0 gives a strong noise contribution in MAME's mixer. 
; Volume byte examples
; A only, loud:
ld  a,$0F
out (VOLAB),a

; B only, loud:
ld  a,$F0
out (VOLAB),a

; A+B balanced:
ld  a,$88
out (VOLAB),a

; C only, medium:
ld  a,$08
out (VOLC),a

Vibrato and noise

Register 4 packs vibrato speed and depth into one byte. The top two bits select a vibrato speed tap, and the bottom six bits set depth. Register 5 chooses whether the master oscillator reload modulation comes from vibrato or noise, and also enables audible noise contribution.

; Vibrato register format
; D7 D6 = speed
; D5-D0 = depth

VIB_SPEED_0 equ %00000000
VIB_SPEED_1 equ %01000000
VIB_SPEED_2 equ %10000000
VIB_SPEED_3 equ %11000000
VIB_DEPTH_MASK equ %00111111

Gentle vibrato

ld  a,%01000100  ; speed 1, depth 4
out (VIBRA),a
ld  a,$00        ; reg 5 D4=0: vibrato mux path
out (VOLC),a

Use this after a stable tone is already audible. Keep depth low first.

Noise burst setup

ld  a,$20        ; noise AM enable, C volume 0
out (VOLC),a
ld  a,$F0        ; strong noise volume value
out (VOLN),a

Good starting point for static, hits, zap textures, or explosions.

Do not overfit the names VOLC is not only “volume C.” It also holds noise AM enable and mux selection bits. That name comes from console-style shorthand, but the register is a packed control register.

The arcade SI/SO caveat

MAME's Astrocade sound-device source makes the arcade difference explicit: the device has active-high SO strobes triggered by read accesses, and while SO and SI logically appear as if they should be tied to the same matrix, that only appears true on the home systems. Arcade games instead channel SI through external multiplexers and connect SO strobes to unrelated outputs that often use upper address bits as data.

Assumption Home console tendency Arcade field-guide rule
Sound and controller matrix are one tidy scheme. Often a useful mental model. Wrong enough to be dangerous for WoW/Gorf-class arcade work.
SO0-SO7 select input rows only. Can match console examples. Arcade SO strobes may drive unrelated cabinet/board outputs.
Reading a sound-side port is harmless. May simply scan input state. Could trigger strobes or mux behavior; treat reads as hardware operations.
Write-only sound recipes are safe. Usually safe. Still the safest baseline for new arcade code.
Practical consequence For new arcade sound effects, prefer OUT-only routines that write registers $10-$17. Do not borrow console routines that read sound/I/O ports unless each read is understood on the specific arcade board.

Tested-style recipes

These are deliberately small, boring, and easy to debug. They are not final Wizard of Wor sound design; they are safe patterns for proving that the sound path works without dragging in console SI/SO behavior.

1. Silence everything

; Silence tone A/B/C and noise contribution.
SoundSilence:
    xor a
    out (VOLAB),a     ; A=0, B=0
    out (VOLC),a      ; C=0, noise AM off, mux vibrato
    out (VOLN),a      ; noise volume 0
    ret

2. Single-channel beep on tone A

; Simple A-channel beep.
; Assumes C4 is defined by HW-HVGLIB.H.h as a useful tone value.
SoundBeepA:
    ld  a,$80         ; moderate master oscillator baseline
    out (TONMO),a
    ld  a,C4
    out (TONEA),a
    xor a
    out (VOLC),a      ; C off, noise AM off
    out (VOLN),a      ; noise off
    ld  a,$0F         ; A loud, B silent
    out (VOLAB),a
    ret

3. Coin-up chirp, two quick pitches

; Blocking demo chirp. Replace DelayShort with your frame/timer delay.
SoundCoinChirp:
    ld  a,$80
    out (TONMO),a
    xor a
    out (VOLC),a
    out (VOLN),a
    ld  a,$0C         ; A medium/loud
    out (VOLAB),a

    ld  a,C5
    out (TONEA),a
    call DelayShort

    ld  a,G4
    out (TONEA),a
    call DelayShort

    xor a
    out (VOLAB),a
    ret

4. Two-tone warning using A and B

SoundTwoTone:
    ld  a,$80
    out (TONMO),a
    ld  a,C4
    out (TONEA),a
    ld  a,G4
    out (TONEB),a
    xor a
    out (VOLC),a
    out (VOLN),a
    ld  a,$88         ; B volume 8, A volume 8
    out (VOLAB),a
    ret

5. Noise zap / explosion seed

SoundNoiseZap:
    ld  a,$60         ; master oscillator baseline
    out (TONMO),a
    xor a
    out (VOLAB),a     ; no A/B tone
    ld  a,$20         ; noise AM enable, C volume 0
    out (VOLC),a
    ld  a,$F0         ; strong noise volume/control
    out (VOLN),a
    ret

6. Siren sweep by software

; Non-blocking game code should update TONEA over time instead of spinning here.
; This blocking version is only a proof-of-sound sketch.
SoundSirenStep:
    ld  a,$80
    out (TONMO),a
    ld  a,$0F
    out (VOLAB),a
    xor a
    out (VOLC),a
    out (VOLN),a
    ; Caller changes A or a RAM variable and writes it to TONEA each frame.
    ret

Debugging sound

Sound bugs are hard because a wrong value might be silent, painfully loud, or routed through a cabinet latch you did not mean to touch. Keep early tests small and visible.

Symptom Likely cause Test
No sound at all All volume nibbles zero, audio board path disabled, or wrong port direction. Write TONMO, TONEA, then VOLAB=$0F.
Harsh stuck tone Volume left on after a blocking test or no duration/silence step. Call SoundSilence after a known delay.
Random weirdness when using console code Console routine reads sound/I/O ports or assumes SO/SI wiring. Remove all IN instructions from the sound routine.
Noise but no pitch Noise AM/volume set, tone volumes zero. Clear VOLC/VOLN, set VOLAB=$0F.
Pitch changes seem inverted Reload values are not linear “higher number = higher pitch” in the simple musical sense. Try the known note constants from the include and document what you hear.
MAME workflow In MAME, break on the routine that starts your sound, step through each OUT, and verify the exact register values. On real hardware, start with short tones and keep volume conservative until the audio path is known good.

Suggested symbols for WoW-targeted code

Use output-specific names in code. That makes it obvious that $11 is a tone write here, not a player-2 input read.

; Output-side sound ports
SND_MASTER      equ PORT_TONMO   ; $10
SND_TONE_A      equ PORT_TONEA   ; $11
SND_TONE_B      equ PORT_TONEB   ; $12
SND_TONE_C      equ PORT_TONEC   ; $13
SND_VIBRATO     equ PORT_VIBRA   ; $14
SND_CTRL_C      equ PORT_VOLC    ; $15
SND_VOL_AB      equ PORT_VOLAB   ; $16
SND_NOISE_VOL   equ PORT_VOLN    ; $17

; Register 5 bits
SND5_NOISE_AM   equ %00100000
SND5_MUX_NOISE  equ %00010000
SND5_C_VOL_MASK equ %00001111

; Register 6 nibbles
SND6_A_VOL_MASK equ %00001111
SND6_B_VOL_MASK equ %11110000

For input-side code, keep using input-specific names like PORT_PLAYER2 and PORT_PLAYER1. Do not let a generic PORT_11 or PORT_12 symbol leak into production code.
