Goal: build up the missing middle layer between technician documentation and working software: a practical, tested, source-backed guide for Astrocade arcade development and understanding.

why is this hard? The Astrocade arcade platform was not a consumer development target. Bally/Midway and Dave Nutting Associates built commercial embedded games, shipped ROMs, and documented the machines mainly so operators could keep them running.

The result is an archaeological dig. BallyAlley has *tons* of great info but most is targeted to the console. The service manual tells you what the board is. MAME tells you how the emulator sees it. The console manuals tell you what the custom chips were meant to do. Robby Roto and Wizard of Wor disassemblies tell you how commercial games actually used them. Real hardware tells you which assumptions survived contact with the cabinet.

Not console Astrocade

The home system knowledge matters, especially Magic, palette, and sound concepts. But arcade machines add card racks, different input routing, pattern boards, protected RAM behavior, speech hardware, and hi-res video assumptions.

Not MAME either

MAME is the best executable specification we have; this guide distinguishes emulator-confirmed behavior from schematic and from real-hardware.

The arcade Astrocade family

The same design lineage appears across multiple Bally/Midway titles, with variations in ROM layout, RAM, input routing, speech, and pattern-board usage.
